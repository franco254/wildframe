Tracking Wolves in Białowieża

January 2026  ·  8 min read  ·  Poland

I arrived in Białowieża in the dark, four hours before sunrise. The forest in winter has a quality of silence that feels earned — not the absence of sound, but the presence of something older than sound. Snow had fallen the previous night, and the tracks I found on the forest path at dawn were still crisp: a large male, moving north.

The quality of waiting that nature photography demands is a different species from impatience. You are not waiting for something to arrive. You are learning to be in a place without needing it to perform.

I spent the first day learning the territory. A fixed hide had been placed near a clearing where the gamekeeper had seen a pack the previous week. I sat in it for nine hours. I saw a woodpecker, three deer, and a man on a bicycle who waved. No wolves.

The second day

It is easy to misunderstand what wildlife photography requires. The technical side — exposure, focus, lens choice — is learnable in an afternoon. What is not learnable is the willingness to be cold, to be patient, to let a day go by with nothing but the gathering dark to show for it.

The wolves came on the third evening, twenty minutes before I had decided to give up and walk back to the car. They did not emerge dramatically. They materialised — three of them, adults, moving through the birch trees at a pace that looked unhurried and was, in fact, very fast. I had 40 seconds. I made 60 frames. Three of them were any good.

That ratio feels about right for this kind of work.

Technical notes

I was shooting with a 500mm prime at f/4, ISO 6400, in light that any reasonable person would describe as inadequate. The camera's subject tracking locked on immediately — a reminder that the tools available now would have seemed miraculous twenty years ago, and still require the photographer to be in the right place first.
